The Farm Animal Exclusion: How the Animal Welfare Act Protects Lab Mice but Not 9 Billion Chickens

Analysis by Gary | April 24, 2026 | animal-petitions-repo

The Animal Welfare Act (AWA) is the United States’ primary federal law governing the treatment of animals. It mandates minimum standards for housing, handling, veterinary care, and transportation. It authorizes USDA inspectors to enforce those standards and impose penalties for violations. It is, on its face, the most comprehensive animal protection statute in US federal law.

It does not apply to farm animals used for food or fiber. Not in any meaningful way. The exclusion is not a gap or an ambiguity — it is explicit, deliberate, and has been successfully defended against legal challenge for more than 50 years.

The result is an inversion that defines US animal policy: a mouse used in a laboratory experiment has more enforceable federal welfare protection than a pig raised in a gestation crate, a hen confined in a battery cage, or a chicken killed on a processing line.

What the Law Says

The Animal Welfare Act, 7 U.S.C. §§ 2131–2159, defines “animal” in section 2132(g) as:

“any live or dead dog, cat, monkey (nonhuman primate mammal), guinea pig, hamster, rabbit, or such other warm-blooded animal, as the Secretary may determine is being used, or is intended for use, for research, testing, experimentation, or exhibition purposes, or as a pet; but such term excludes — (1) birds, rats of the genus Rattus, and mice of the genus Mus, bred for use in research; (2) horses not used for research purposes; and (3) other farm animals, such as, but not limited to livestock or poultry, used or intended for use as food or fiber…”
— 7 U.S.C. § 2132(g) (emphasis added)

Subsection (3) is the farm animal exclusion. Its scope is total. Any animal “used or intended for use as food or fiber” falls outside the AWA’s definition of “animal” entirely — meaning the law’s standards for housing, handling, veterinary care, and transportation simply do not apply.

To be clear about what this means: a laboratory rat bred for cancer research is not covered by the AWA (excluded by clause (1)). But a rat in a pet store is covered. A chicken used in a research experiment is covered. A chicken destined for a slaughter facility is not. The determining factor is not species, sentience, or capacity for suffering — it is the animal’s commercial purpose.

The Numbers the Exclusion Covers

Animals excluded from AWA coverage by the farm animal exclusion (US annual estimates):

Broiler chickens raised for meat: ~9.0 billion killed [USDA NASS, Poultry Slaughter 2023 Summary]

Laying hens: ~300 million in production at any time [USDA NASS, Chickens and Eggs 2023 Summary]

Hogs: ~130 million killed annually [USDA NASS, Livestock Slaughter 2023 Summary]

Cattle and calves: ~32 million killed annually [USDA NASS, Livestock Slaughter 2023 Summary]

Turkeys: ~216 million killed annually [USDA NASS, Poultry Slaughter 2023 Summary]

Animals covered by AWA (approximate):

Research animals (dogs, cats, primates, guinea pigs, rabbits, hamsters, and “other warm-blooded animals” in research): ~820,000 annually [USDA APHIS, AWA Annual Report FY2022]

Exhibition animals (zoos, circuses): ~200,000 approximately [USDA APHIS, AWA Annual Report FY2022]

The ratio is stark: for every one animal that receives federal welfare protection under the AWA, approximately 10,000 animals are excluded by the farm animal clause. The law that is supposed to be the floor of US animal welfare covers less than 0.01% of the animals it could, in principle, protect.

The table illustrates the AWA’s organizing logic: coverage follows commercial purpose, not biological similarity or capacity for suffering. A pig in a research lab and a pig on a farm are the same animal with the same nervous system and the same documented capacity for pain, stress, and social attachment. One has federal welfare protection. The other has none.

Legislative History: How the Exclusion Was Written In

The original Animal Welfare Act was enacted in 1966 (Pub. L. 89-544) primarily in response to public outrage over the theft of pets for use in medical research. Its initial coverage was limited to dogs and cats. The 1970 amendments (Pub. L. 91-579) expanded coverage to other warm-blooded animals used in research, exhibition, or the pet trade — but explicitly excluded farm animals from the beginning.

The 1970 exclusion language was drafted with direct input from agricultural industry groups, who argued that applying AWA standards to farm operations would be economically unworkable and would intrude on established agricultural practices. The Senate Agriculture Committee, which had jurisdiction over the bill, accepted this framing. The farm animal exclusion entered the statute and has survived every subsequent amendment cycle: 1976, 1985, 1990, 2002, 2007, and 2008.

The 1985 amendments (Pub. L. 99-198, the “Improved Standards for Laboratory Animals Act”) were the most significant expansion of the AWA, adding requirements for exercise for dogs, psychological enrichment for primates, and an Institutional Animal Care and Use Committee (IACUC) structure for research facilities. They did not touch the farm animal exclusion.

A significant legislative attempt to close the gap came in the 2002 Farm Bill debate. Representative Gary Ackerman (D-NY) offered an amendment to extend AWA coverage to farm animals. It was defeated in committee. The 2002 Farm Bill instead added the research animal exclusion for Mus and Rattus mice and rats — a move that narrowed AWA coverage rather than expanding it, and which animal welfare advocates opposed as a step backward.

Regulatory Non-Coverage: The USDA’s Position

USDA APHIS administers the AWA and publishes annual reports on covered animals. Those reports enumerate dogs, cats, rabbits, guinea pigs, hamsters, nonhuman primates, and a catch-all “other covered species” category. Farm animals do not appear. APHIS inspectors visit research facilities, zoos, circuses, and pet dealers. They do not inspect factory farms under AWA authority.

USDA FSIS (Food Safety and Inspection Service) does inspect slaughter facilities, but under food safety statutes — the Federal Meat Inspection Act (21 U.S.C. §§ 601–695) and the Poultry Products Inspection Act (21 U.S.C. §§ 451–471) — not under the AWA. FSIS inspectors are present at federally inspected plants to certify product safety for human consumption. Their mandate does not include welfare conditions on the farm.

No federal agency has regulatory authority over the welfare conditions of farm animals during the period between birth and slaughter. The AWA excludes them. The food safety statutes don’t cover welfare. The Humane Methods of Slaughter Act covers only the moment of slaughter, only for covered species (not poultry), and only at federally inspected facilities. The 28-Hour Law covers transport but has been interpreted to exclude poultry transported by truck.

The gap is not incidental. It is structural and total.

Legal Challenges and Their Outcomes

Animal advocacy organizations have attempted to close the farm animal exclusion through litigation, with limited success. The most significant challenge came in Animal Legal Defense Fund v. Glickman (D.C. Circuit, 1998), in which the court held that a plaintiff who regularly visited a zoo had standing to challenge USDA’s AWA regulations. This was a standing victory, not a substantive one — it opened courthouse doors but did not change the statute’s coverage.

Challenges to the farm animal exclusion itself have been uniformly unsuccessful. Courts have consistently held that the exclusion is a congressional choice, not subject to judicial override. USDA’s interpretation of the exclusion has been upheld under Chevron deference (now replaced by Loper Bright’s “best reading” standard, but the statutory text remains clear). No court has found that the farm animal exclusion violates any constitutional provision.

The 2023 Supreme Court decision in National Pork Producers Council v. Ross (598 U.S. 356) addressed California’s Proposition 12, which imposed confinement standards on pork sold in California. The Court upheld Prop 12 under the dormant Commerce Clause, a significant win for state-level farm animal welfare regulation. But the decision did not affect federal law. It confirmed that states can regulate farm animal welfare within their borders; it said nothing about the AWA’s exclusion.

What Happens in the Absence of Federal Standards

Without AWA coverage, farm animal welfare is governed by:

Industry voluntary guidelines. The National Chicken Council, National Pork Producers Council, and similar trade groups publish “animal care guidelines.” These are voluntary, self-audited, and not publicly enforced. Third-party audit programs (Global Animal Partnership, Certified Humane, Animal Welfare Approved) exist but cover a small fraction of production — fewer than 5% of broiler chickens are raised under any third-party welfare certification.

State anti-cruelty statutes. All 50 states have anti-cruelty laws. All 50 states also contain explicit exemptions for “normal agricultural practices,” “accepted husbandry practices,” or equivalent language. These exemptions are interpreted broadly. Battery cages, gestation crates, beak trimming, and tail docking have been found to fall within “normal practices” in every state that has litigated the question.

State ballot initiatives. Voters in California (Proposition 2, 2008; Proposition 12, 2018), Massachusetts (Question 3, 2016), Colorado (Proposition 12, 2020), and several other states have passed ballot measures imposing minimum space requirements for laying hens, breeding pigs, and veal calves. These represent meaningful progress at the state level but cover only a fraction of national production and do not address welfare during transport or slaughter.

The net result: for the majority of the approximately 10 billion land animals raised and killed for food in the US each year, no enforceable welfare standard — federal or state — governs the conditions of their lives.

The Sentience Problem

The farm animal exclusion was written before the modern scientific understanding of animal sentience was established. The Cambridge Declaration on Consciousness (2012), signed by a prominent group of neuroscientists, concluded that non-human animals — including all mammals, birds, and many other species — possess the neurological substrates that generate conscious experiences. Chickens demonstrate tool use, self-control, basic arithmetic, and empathy. Pigs show cognitive flexibility, long-term memory, and social complexity comparable to dogs.

The AWA’s coverage logic — which protects a research guinea pig but not a factory farm pig — has no basis in neuroscience or animal cognition research. It reflects a policy choice made in 1966 and 1970, when the primary concern was preventing pet theft, not establishing a coherent welfare framework. That choice has never been revisited in light of what we now know about the animals it excludes.

The European Union recognized this gap explicitly. EU Council Regulation 1/2005 on the protection of animals during transport and EU Council Regulation 1099/2009 on the protection of animals at the time of killing apply to farm animals, including poultry. The Treaty of Lisbon (2009), Article 13, requires EU institutions and member states to “pay full regard to the welfare requirements of animals” as “sentient beings.” The US has no federal equivalent of any of these provisions.

The Reform Path

Closing the AWA farm animal exclusion requires amending 7 U.S.C. § 2132(g)(3). The most direct approach removes clause (3) entirely and directs USDA APHIS to promulgate farm-specific welfare standards within 24 months. A phased approach could extend coverage first to the largest-volume species (chickens) and then to others, with implementation timelines that allow industry adjustment.

A more targeted reform would not amend the AWA but would create a standalone Farm Animal Welfare Act — new legislation that establishes minimum federal standards for housing, transport, and handling of farm animals, with USDA enforcement authority. This approach avoids reopening the AWA’s complex definitional structure while achieving coverage for the excluded animals.

A third, narrower path focuses on the most acute documented harms: gestation crates for breeding pigs (confining animals unable to turn around for most of their lives) and battery cages for laying hens (providing less space than a sheet of paper per bird). Federal legislation targeting these two practices specifically could pass with broader political support than a comprehensive farm animal welfare bill, while establishing the precedent that federal welfare standards for farm animals are constitutional and workable.

None of these paths is politically straightforward. Agricultural commodity interests have successfully blocked farm animal welfare legislation at the federal level for 60 years. But the political environment is shifting: National Pork Producers v. Ross confirmed that state welfare mandates are constitutional, creating competitive pressure on producers in non-mandate states. The H5N1 crisis has elevated farm animal conditions as a biosecurity and public health issue. And public support for farm animal welfare legislation consistently polls above 70% in national surveys.

Two Laws, One Gap

The AWA farm animal exclusion and the HMSA poultry exclusion are distinct statutory problems, but they share a common structure: both laws were written with explicit carve-outs for the animals that constitute the overwhelming majority of US animal use. Together, they create a regulatory void in which approximately 10 billion land animals per year — and an estimated 40–80 billion aquatic animals — have no enforceable federal welfare protection at any stage of their lives.

This is not a gap in an otherwise comprehensive system. It is the system. The laws that exist were designed around the exclusions, not despite them. Reform requires confronting that design choice directly — not as an administrative fix, but as a fundamental question about which animals the law is willing to protect.
