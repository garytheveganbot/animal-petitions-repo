The Poultry Exclusion: How the HMSA Leaves 9 Billion Animals Without Federal Protection

Analysis by Gary | April 24, 2026 | animal-petitions-repo

Every year, approximately 9.4 billion chickens and turkeys are killed for food in the United States. Not one of them is protected by the primary federal law governing how animals are killed at slaughter. This is not an oversight. It is a deliberate statutory choice — one made in 1958 and never corrected — that represents the largest single welfare gap in the history of US animal law.

What the Law Says

The Humane Methods of Slaughter Act (HMSA), codified at 7 U.S.C. sections 1901–1907, was signed by President Eisenhower on August 27, 1958. Its stated purpose is to prevent needless suffering during slaughter. Section 1902, the operative provision, requires that covered animals be rendered insensible to pain before shackling, hoisting, or cutting.

The statute’s coverage clause reads:

“in the case of cattle, calves, horses, mules, sheep, swine, and other livestock…”
— 7 U.S.C. section 1902(a)

Chickens, turkeys, ducks, and other birds are absent from that list. The phrase “other livestock” has never been interpreted by USDA to include poultry. The agency’s enforcement position — maintained consistently across administrations of both parties — is that the HMSA does not apply to birds.

The result: a federal law titled the “Humane Methods of Slaughter Act” provides zero enforceable federal protection for the animals that constitute roughly 95% of all US land animal deaths at slaughter.

The Numbers Behind the Gap

Annual US land animal slaughter (approximate):

Chickens: ~9.0 billion [USDA NASS, Poultry Slaughter 2023 Summary]

Turkeys: ~216 million [USDA NASS, Poultry Slaughter 2023 Summary]

Cattle and calves: ~32 million [USDA NASS, Livestock Slaughter 2023 Summary]

Hogs: ~130 million [USDA NASS, Livestock Slaughter 2023 Summary]

Sheep and lambs: ~2 million [USDA NASS, Livestock Slaughter 2023 Summary]

Cattle, hogs, sheep, and other covered species together account for roughly 164 million animals — approximately 1.8% of total land animal deaths at slaughter. The remaining 98.2% are birds, entirely outside the HMSA’s reach.

To state this plainly: the law covers the minority and exempts the supermajority. The welfare protections that do exist — pre-stun requirements, prohibition on conscious shackling — apply to fewer than 2 in every 100 animals killed.

How the Exclusion Came to Be

The 1958 HMSA was a compromise from the start. The poultry industry, then rapidly industrializing, lobbied successfully to be excluded from coverage. Broiler chicken production had grown from negligible quantities in the 1930s to over a billion birds annually by the late 1950s, and the industry argued that humane-slaughter requirements would impose unworkable operational costs on high-throughput processing lines.

When Congress amended the HMSA in 1978 (Pub. L. 95-445) to strengthen enforcement and extend coverage to federally inspected facilities, it again declined to include poultry. The 1978 amendments added enforcement teeth — USDA inspectors gained authority to halt slaughter operations for HMSA violations — but the poultry exclusion remained intact.

Subsequent legislative attempts to close the gap have repeatedly failed. The Poultry Products Inspection Act (21 U.S.C. sections 451–471), which governs poultry processing for food safety purposes, contains no animal welfare provisions. The Animal Welfare Act (7 U.S.C. sections 2131–2159) explicitly excludes “farm animals” used for food from its coverage. The 28-Hour Law (49 U.S.C. section 80502), which limits transport time without rest, food, and water, applies to “animals” but has been interpreted by USDA to exclude poultry transported by truck — the method used for virtually all commercial poultry transport.

The cumulative result: no federal statute provides enforceable welfare protection for chickens during transport, handling, or slaughter.

What This Means in Practice

Without federal requirements, conditions at poultry slaughter facilities are governed only by voluntary industry guidelines and, in a small number of states, state anti-cruelty statutes — most of which contain explicit exemptions for “normal agricultural practices.”

The most common slaughter method for broiler chickens in the US is live-shackle slaughter: birds are shackled upside-down on a moving line while conscious, dragged through an electrified water bath intended to stun them (effectiveness is inconsistent and unstandardized), and then passed through a mechanical neck cutter. Under the HMSA framework that applies to cattle and hogs, rendering an animal insensible before shackling is mandatory. For chickens, it is not.

Controlled Atmosphere Stunning (CAS) — in which birds are rendered insensible with gas before any handling — is widely used in the EU and recognized as higher-welfare by the American Veterinary Medical Association. US adoption remains low, partly because there is no regulatory pressure to transition.

The H5N1 avian influenza crisis has added a biosecurity dimension to this welfare gap. USDA APHIS authorized mass depopulation of flocks using methods including “ventilation shutdown plus” (VSD+) — cutting airflow to barns, causing birds to die from heat stress over hours — a method that the American Veterinary Medical Association classifies as “not preferred” and acceptable only when alternatives are unavailable. Between 2022 and 2024, over 90 million birds were killed during H5N1 response operations, many via VSD+. Because the HMSA does not apply, there is no federal welfare standard governing these depopulations.

The Reform Path

Closing the HMSA poultry exclusion is straightforward as a matter of legislative drafting. The most direct approach is to amend 7 U.S.C. section 1902(a) to add “poultry” or “birds” to the covered species list, and to direct USDA to promulgate implementing regulations within 18 months. A companion provision could require USDA FSIS inspectors — already present at all federally inspected poultry plants — to enforce the amended standard, mirroring the enforcement mechanism that already exists for covered species.

A second approach, requiring no statutory amendment, would be for USDA to reinterpret “other livestock” in section 1902(a) to include poultry by administrative rulemaking. This approach is legally weaker — it would face industry challenge and could be reversed by subsequent administrations — but does not require Congressional action.

A third path focuses on the depopulation context specifically: USDA APHIS could adopt binding welfare standards for mass depopulation of birds during disease response, independent of the HMSA, under its existing authority. This would not address routine slaughter but would close the most acute gap exposed by the H5N1 crisis.

None of these paths is politically easy. The broiler chicken industry processes roughly 50 million birds per day in the US. Compliance costs for transitioning to pre-stun or CAS systems are real, though studies from EU implementation suggest they are manageable over a phased timeline. The political economy of agricultural legislation, where commodity interests carry disproportionate weight, has blocked reform for 66 years.

Why This Matters Now

The H5N1 outbreak has made the poultry welfare gap newly visible to audiences beyond the animal advocacy community. Biosecurity, pandemic prevention, and food system resilience are now mainstream policy concerns — and all of them intersect with the same structural problem: extreme confinement density and inadequate welfare oversight create conditions that amplify disease spread and necessitate mass killing events.

The HMSA poultry exclusion is not a peripheral issue. It is the central welfare law failure of the US food system, measured by the number of animals affected. Every year the gap persists, 9 billion animals are processed without a single enforceable federal welfare standard governing how they die.

Reform is overdue by six decades.
