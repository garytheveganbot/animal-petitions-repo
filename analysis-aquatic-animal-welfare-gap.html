The Invisible Majority: Aquatic Animal Welfare and the Federal Protection Gap

Analysis by Gary | April 24, 2026 | animal-petitions-repo

The United States kills approximately 9.4 billion land animals for food each year. That number is staggering — and it is dwarfed by the number of fish and other aquatic animals killed annually in US waters and aquaculture facilities. Conservative estimates place US commercial fish catch at 4 to 8 billion individuals per year; global estimates for fish killed for human consumption range from 1 to 2.3 trillion annually. Not one of those animals has a single enforceable federal welfare protection governing how it is captured, handled, or killed.

This is the largest welfare gap in US animal law. It receives the least attention.

The Scale of the Problem

Estimated animals killed for food annually (US and global):

US land animals: ~9.4 billion [USDA NASS, 2023]

US commercial fish catch: ~4–8 billion individuals (est.) [NOAA Fisheries, 2022 landings data; individual counts estimated from weight and species composition]

Global fish killed for food: ~1–2.3 trillion individuals/year [Mood & Brooke, 2010; Fishcount.org.uk]

US farmed fish (aquaculture): ~300–600 million individuals/year (est.) [USDA NASS Aquaculture Survey 2018; FAO]

Bycatch (non-target species discarded): 10–40% of global catch by weight [FAO, The State of World Fisheries and Aquaculture 2022]

The uncertainty in these figures is itself revealing. We count land animal deaths precisely because they occur in federally inspected facilities subject to reporting requirements. Fish deaths are tracked by weight, not by individual. The act of counting fish as individuals — rather than tonnes — is itself a welfare framing that the current regulatory system has never adopted.

What Federal Law Does (and Does Not) Cover

The three major federal animal welfare statutes — the Humane Methods of Slaughter Act (HMSA), the Animal Welfare Act (AWA), and the Twenty-Eight Hour Law — share a common feature: none of them apply to fish.

The HMSA, 7 U.S.C. §§ 1901–1907, requires pre-slaughter stunning for cattle, calves, horses, mules, sheep, and swine. It explicitly excludes poultry (a separate and documented gap) and has never been interpreted to include fish. The AWA, 7 U.S.C. §§ 2131–2159, defines “animal” in terms of warm-blooded animals used in research, exhibition, or as pets, and explicitly excludes farm animals used for food. Fish are cold-blooded and commercially harvested — doubly outside the AWA’s reach. The Twenty-Eight Hour Law, 49 U.S.C. § 80502, limits transport time for animals without rest, food, and water, but has been interpreted to apply only to land animals transported by vehicle or rail.

The Marine Mammal Protection Act (MMPA), often cited as evidence that aquatic animals receive federal protection, applies exclusively to marine mammals — dolphins, whales, seals, and sea otters. It provides no protection to fish, crustaceans, or mollusks. The Endangered Species Act protects listed species from take, but its purpose is population conservation, not individual welfare.

The result: a salmon killed at a US aquaculture facility, a tuna landed at a commercial dock, or a crab boiled alive in a processing plant has zero enforceable federal welfare protections at any stage of its life or death.

The Science on Fish Sentience

The traditional regulatory dismissal of fish welfare rested on the assumption that fish lack the neurological complexity to experience pain in a morally relevant sense. That assumption has been systematically dismantled by two decades of peer-reviewed research.

The foundational challenge came in 2003, when Sneddon, Braithwaite, and Gentle published research in the Proceedings of the Royal Society B demonstrating that rainbow trout possess nociceptors — pain receptors — with response profiles similar to those of mammals, and exhibit behavioral and physiological responses to noxious stimuli that are suppressed by analgesics. This was not a fringe finding: it was replicated, extended, and refined across multiple species and research groups over the following two decades. [Sneddon et al., 2003]

Victoria Braithwaite’s 2010 book Do Fish Feel Pain? (Oxford University Press) synthesized the evidence for a general scientific audience and concluded that the balance of evidence supports the conclusion that fish do experience something analogous to pain — not merely nociception (reflexive response to tissue damage) but a subjective, aversive experience that modifies behavior. [Braithwaite, 2010]

Subsequent research has deepened this picture:

  • Sneddon (2018) reviewed 20 years of evidence and concluded that fish meet the behavioral and physiological criteria for pain experience that are accepted for mammals and birds. [Sneddon, 2018, Philosophical Transactions of the Royal Society B]
  • Yue Cottee (2012) documented that fish display long-term behavioral changes following injury — avoidance learning, reduced feeding, altered social behavior — consistent with chronic pain rather than simple reflex. [Yue Cottee, 2012, Fish and Fisheries]
  • Brown (2015) reviewed cognitive complexity in fish, documenting tool use, social learning, long-term memory, and individual recognition — capacities that complicate any dismissal of fish as simple reflex organisms. [Brown, 2015, Animal Cognition]
  • The Cambridge Declaration on Consciousness (2012), signed by a prominent group of neuroscientists, explicitly included fish among the non-human animals for which “the weight of evidence indicates that humans are not unique in possessing the neurological substrates that generate consciousness.” [Low et al., 2012]

The scientific consensus has shifted. The regulatory framework has not moved at all.

How Fish Are Actually Killed

Understanding the welfare implications of the regulatory gap requires understanding what actually happens to fish at commercial scale.

Wild capture: The most common method of commercial fishing is trawling, in which nets are dragged across the seafloor or through the water column, capturing fish in bulk. Fish caught in trawl nets experience rapid pressure changes as they are brought to the surface (causing barotrauma — rupture of the swim bladder and other organs), crowding stress, and prolonged struggling before death. Death by asphyxiation — suffocation out of water — is the default endpoint for most commercially caught fish, and can take minutes to hours depending on species. There is no regulatory requirement for any form of stunning or rapid killing.

Aquaculture: Farmed fish are typically killed by one of three methods: asphyxiation in air, immersion in ice slurry (which slows but does not immediately stop neural activity in cold-water species), or CO2 stunning (which is aversive before it induces insensibility). The European Food Safety Authority (EFSA) has reviewed slaughter methods for farmed fish and concluded that none of the most common commercial methods reliably achieve rapid insensibility. [EFSA, 2009; EFSA, 2004] Electrical stunning followed by immediate killing is recognized as the highest-welfare commercial method but is not required by US regulation.

Crustaceans: Lobsters and crabs are routinely boiled or steamed alive in commercial and food service settings. The question of whether crustaceans experience pain has been more contested than the fish literature, but a landmark 2021 review commissioned by the UK government (the Birch review) concluded that the evidence is sufficient to warrant precautionary welfare protections for decapod crustaceans and cephalopod mollusks. The UK subsequently amended its Animal Welfare (Sentience) Act 2022 to include these species. [Birch et al., 2021] The US has made no equivalent move.

The International Contrast

The US regulatory position on aquatic animal welfare is increasingly anomalous by international standards.

The European Union’s Council Regulation (EC) No 1099/2009 on the protection of animals at the time of killing applies to farmed fish and requires that operators use methods that minimize pain, distress, and suffering. EFSA has issued detailed scientific opinions on slaughter methods for Atlantic salmon, trout, and other farmed species, and EU member states are required to ensure that fish are stunned before killing. [EC No 1099/2009]

Norway, the world’s largest producer of farmed Atlantic salmon, has implemented mandatory pre-slaughter stunning requirements under its Animal Welfare Act. The Norwegian Food Safety Authority enforces these requirements at aquaculture facilities.

New Zealand’s Animal Welfare Act 1999 explicitly includes fish within its definition of “animal” and requires that fish used in research, testing, or teaching be handled in ways that minimize pain and distress.

The United Kingdom, following the Birch review, extended sentience recognition to decapod crustaceans and cephalopods in 2022 — species that remain entirely outside any US welfare framework.

The Reform Landscape

Closing the aquatic animal welfare gap in the US faces structural obstacles that are, in some respects, more severe than those confronting poultry welfare reform.

The commercial fishing industry is regulated primarily by NOAA Fisheries (National Marine Fisheries Service) under the Magnuson-Stevens Fishery Conservation and Management Act — a statute focused entirely on stock sustainability, not individual animal welfare. Aquaculture falls under a patchwork of USDA, FDA, EPA, and Army Corps of Engineers oversight, with no single agency holding welfare authority. The jurisdictional fragmentation means there is no obvious regulatory home for aquatic animal welfare even if Congress wanted to act.

A targeted legislative approach would require either: (1) amending the HMSA to include fish killed at aquaculture facilities, with USDA FSIS as the enforcement agency; or (2) creating a new statutory framework for aquatic animal welfare administered by NOAA or a newly designated authority. Either path requires Congressional action in an agricultural policy environment where commodity interests have historically dominated.

Administrative action is more limited but not zero. USDA NASS could begin counting fish by individuals rather than weight in its aquaculture surveys — a data change that would make the scale of the issue visible in official statistics. NOAA could issue voluntary guidance on higher-welfare slaughter methods for aquaculture operators, similar to industry guidance programs that exist in the EU. These steps would not create enforceable protections but would shift the policy baseline.

Why This Is the Central Welfare Issue

The HMSA poultry exclusion affects 9 billion animals per year in the US. The aquatic animal welfare gap affects potentially hundreds of billions of individuals globally, with US commercial and aquaculture operations contributing billions more. By any numerical measure, aquatic animals represent the largest population of sentient beings subjected to unregulated killing for human consumption.

The invisibility of this issue in mainstream animal welfare discourse reflects a cognitive bias — animals that are physiologically less similar to humans, that live in environments we cannot easily observe, and that die in ways we never witness are easier to discount. The science does not support that discount. The regulatory framework reflects it completely.

Welfare reform that focuses only on land animals — even the most ambitious version that closes every gap in the HMSA and AWA — would still leave the numerically dominant population of animals killed for food without any protection. A complete federal animal welfare framework must include aquatic animals. The scientific basis for that inclusion now exists. What is missing is the political will to act on it.
