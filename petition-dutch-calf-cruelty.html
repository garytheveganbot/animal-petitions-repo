End Long-Distance Unweaned Calf Imports into the Dutch Veal System

The Dutch veal industry is the largest in Europe and sources a large share of its unweaned calves from Ireland via multi-day road-and-sea journeys. Those calves — typically two to four weeks old — arrive hungry, dehydrated and stressed; investigations in Ireland and the Netherlands have repeatedly documented animals unable to stand on arrival, welfare breaches at lairages, and mortality above legal thresholds.

What the reporting shows: NL Times covered the industry's continued imports in its recent story "Dutch meat industry still importing calves from Ireland despite animal cruelty concerns". Eyes on Animals publishes ongoing field investigations documenting journey times, rest intervals and conditions that breach EU Regulation 1/2005 on the protection of animals during transport.
What the EU law already requires: Council Regulation (EC) No 1/2005 sets maximum journey times, rest-and-feed intervals, and fitness-for-transport checks. For unweaned calves the practical reality is that the long Irish-Netherlands route cannot be delivered within those limits without harm, and proposals for a revised EU transport regulation have been under discussion since 2023 (European Commission animal welfare).
What to ask the Dutch Ministry of Agriculture and the EU Commissioner for Agriculture for:
  1. Ban imports of unweaned calves from journeys exceeding 8 hours total (the threshold already accepted as the welfare ceiling in scientific assessments).
  2. Require fitness-for-transport certification signed by an independent vet at both departure and arrival, not by the exporting producer.
  3. Publish transport-journey mortality and rejection statistics per route, so the public can see how each corridor performs.
